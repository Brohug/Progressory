## Main Direction

Guard retention should likely not be modeled as just a long flat list of movements.

It works better as a combination of:

- `Core concepts / fundamentals`
- `Shared conceptual themes`
- `Major retention techniques / movements`
- `Shared technique themes`
- `Study categories`
- and the contrast with `Guard Passing`

That matters because many retention actions are not isolated techniques so much as recurring jobs:

- frame
- post
- square back up
- rotate hips back in front
- pummel legs back inside
- rebuild barriers
- recover a usable guard before the pass stabilizes

## Shared Themes In Guard Retention Concepts

A lot of the fundamentals are different words for related jobs.

### A. Structure And Survival Theme

Used to avoid getting flattened, crossfaced, or pinned.

- `Frames`
- `Posts`
- `Structure`
- `Posture Recovery`
- `Base Recovery`
- `Spine Alignment`
- `Neck Safety`
- `Anti-Smash Alignment`
- `Survival Before Offense`

Shared idea:

- stay structurally safe long enough to rebuild guard

### B. Hip Movement And Mobility Theme

Used to create space and move your body back in front of the passer.

- `Hip Mobility`
- `Shrimping`
- `Hip Escape`
- `Shoulder Mobility`
- `Inversion`
- `Granby Movement`
- `Hip Heist`
- `Continuous Movement`

Shared idea:

- your hips must move freely or your guard dies

### C. Barrier And Connection Theme

Used to stop the passer from getting direct access to the torso and hips.

- `Knee-Elbow Connection`
- `Elbow-Knee Connection`
- `Shin-To-Shin Barrier`
- `Foot-To-Hip Connection`
- `Hook Placement`
- `Layered Barriers`
- `Frame Replacement`
- `Frame-To-Hook Conversion`
- `Hook-To-Guard Conversion`

Shared idea:

- use legs and arms as layers that keep the passer away

### D. Space And Alignment Theme

Used to prevent the passer from winning the inside lanes.

- `Inside Position`
- `Distance Management`
- `Range Recognition`
- `Angle Recovery`
- `Squaring Back Up`
- `Hip Line Protection`
- `Knee Line Protection`
- `Inside Space Denial`
- `Retention On Angles`
- `Weak-Side Awareness`

Shared idea:

- do not let the passer get past your hips and knees in a clean line

### E. Grip And Control Denial Theme

Used to stop the passer's connection and pressure from becoming stable.

- `Hand Fighting`
- `Grip Breaking`
- `Wedge Removal`
- `Anti-Crossface Awareness`
- `Anti-Underhook Awareness`
- `Head Position Awareness`
- `Managing Shoulder Line`
- `Connection Management`

Shared idea:

- strip away the controls that make the pass stick

### F. Recovery And Reset Theme

Used when the guard is already compromised and must be rebuilt.

- `Re-Guarding`
- `Guard Recovery`
- `Recovery To Seated Guard`
- `Recovery To Supine Guard`
- `Guard Recomposition`
- `Resetting Structure`
- `Transition Awareness`

Shared idea:

- even if the guard is losing, rebuild a version of it before full control is established

### G. Timing And Feel Theme

Used because retention is reactive and often happens in transitions.

- `Timing`
- `Sensitivity`
- `Reaction-Based Movement`
- `Tension And Relaxation Balance`
- `Mobility Before Control`

- retention works best when you react early and with the right amount of movement

## Shared Themes In Retention Techniques

Many techniques are different, but they group together.

### A. Hip Escape And Framing Theme

Used when the passer is trying to close distance and flatten you.

- `Shrimp`
- `Hip Escape`
- `Reverse Shrimp`
- `Frame-And-Shrimp Recovery`
- `Hip Frame To Knee Insert`
- `Knee Pull-Back Recovery`

Shared idea:

- make space first, then bring your knees back in front

### B. Inversion And Rotation Theme

Used when the passer beats your outside frames and starts getting around your legs.

- `Inversion`
- `Granby Roll`
- `Invert-To-Reguard`
- `Granby-To-Guard Recovery`
- `Shoulder Invert Recovery`
- `Hip Swivel Recovery`

Shared idea:

- rotate your body so your hips and legs can face the passer again

### C. Pummeling And Hook Recovery Theme

Used to reinsert legs and hooks once the passer starts clearing them.

- `Reverse Hook Recovery`

Shared idea:

- win the inside lane back with your legs before the pass settles

### D. Frame-To-Guard Recomposition Theme

## Most Important Guard Retention Fundamentals In Plain Language

If reduced to the most important essentials:

- keep frames between you and the passer
- keep your knees close enough to reconnect to your elbows
- protect your hip line
- protect inside position
- recover angle before the passer settles
- move your hips early
- pummel your legs back inside
- do not stay flat if you can rotate
- remove crossfaces and underhooks early
- replace broken frames immediately
- square back up when you get misaligned
- rebuild any guard before trying to attack
- use layers, not one barrier
- keep moving until structure is restored

## Simple Master View Of Why Guard Retention Works

Most good retention is one of these:

- keep frames in front
- bring knees back inside
- rotate so the hips face the passer again
- pummel hooks back in
- recover distance
- replace a broken guard with another guard before control is established

That is why many different retention techniques feel different but are often built on the same core ideas.

## Very Short Passing Vs Retention Contrast

This is useful as a conceptual anchor:

- `Guard Passing`
  - remove barriers
  - control hips / knees
  - stabilize past the legs

- `Guard Retention`
  - rebuild barriers
  - recover hips / knees in front
  - prevent stabilization

So many passing and retention ideas are direct opposites:

- passer wants inside position / defender wants to deny it
- passer wants hip control / defender wants hip mobility
- passer wants chest-to-chest / defender wants frames and angle
- passer wants to pin legs / defender wants to pummel legs free

## Important Modeling Note

Guard retention should probably not be built as one giant flat list of:

- concepts
- movements
- guard rebuilds
- study categories

Safer structure:

1. `Concept / Fundamentals` layer
2. `Movement / Retention Tool` layer
3. `Specific Guard Recomposition` layer
4. `Shared themes` as the conceptual glue
5. `Passing vs retention` contrast as a teaching aid

This helps the Index support:

- practical retention systems
- conceptual understanding
- clearer anti-passing relationships
- future `Decision Tree` or positional-mapping work
