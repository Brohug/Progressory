## Shared Themes And Families

### A. Straight Foot Lock Family

These attacks mainly target the ankle / Achilles / foot extension line.

- `Straight Ankle Lock`
- `Belly-Down Ankle Lock`

Shared theme:

- controlling the foot
- extending through the ankle
- keeping the knee line trapped
- using hip extension and forearm / wrist placement for pressure

Important note:

`Belly-Down Ankle Lock` is best understood as a major finishing variation of the straight ankle lock rather than a totally separate family, but it is distinct enough that people usually name it separately.

### B. Rotational Foot Lock Family

These attacks mainly target the foot and ankle through twisting pressure.

- `Aoki Lock`
- `Estima Lock`
- `Toe Hold`

Shared theme:

- isolating the foot
- rotating the ankle
- trapping the knee line so the leg cannot follow safely
- creating torsion instead of mostly straight extension

Differences inside this family:

- `Toe Hold` is the classic rotational foot lock
- `Aoki Lock` is a modern rotational foot / ankle attack with a distinct grip and breaking line
- `Estima Lock` is a quick rotational foot lock usually applied from a standing or top entanglement context

### C. Heel Hook Family

These attacks mainly target the knee through heel rotation.

- `Inside Heel Hook`
- `Outside Heel Hook`
- `Lachy Lock`
- `Mikey Lock`
- `Abe Lock`
- `Junny Lock`

Shared theme:

- exposing the heel
- controlling the knee line
- rotating the heel so force transfers into the knee

Main difference:

- `Inside Heel Hook` and `Outside Heel Hook` describe the direction / configuration of rotational breaking pressure

Important note:

These are not duplicates. They are separate core heel hook types.

### D. Knee Extension Family

These attacks mainly target the knee joint through extension.

- `Kneebar`

Shared theme:

This is the leg equivalent of an armbar:

- isolate the leg
- control above and below the knee
- extend hips against the joint line

### E. Compression / Slicer Family

These attacks mainly use a wedge to compress soft tissue and force joint compromise.

- `Calf Slicer`
- `Hamstring Slicer`
- `Texas Cloverleaf`

Shared theme:

- folding the leg over a wedge
- compressing muscle and connective tissue
- sometimes threatening the knee as secondary damage

Differences inside the family:

- `Calf Slicer` focuses lower on the calf
- `Hamstring Slicer` attacks higher behind the knee / hamstring line
- `Texas Cloverleaf` is a more controlling figure-four style leg entanglement that can create strong compression and twisting pressure

### F. Groin / Hip Split Family

These attacks mainly target the groin, hips, and inner-thigh line.

- `Banana Split`
- `Electric Chair`

Shared theme:

- separating the legs
- forcing extreme hip abduction
- using control of one or both legs to stretch the groin and hips

Differences inside the family:

- `Banana Split` is a classic split-style submission
- `Electric Chair` is often taught from half guard and can act as both a sweep and a submission

## Why These Are Separate From The Submission List

These are mostly:

- entries
- control positions
- entanglement structures
- finishing platforms

They are not the submission itself.

For example:

- `Saddle / Honey Hole / 411` usually lead into heel hooks, Aoki locks, toe holds, and kneebars
- `50/50` may lead to straight ankle locks, heel hooks, and Aoki locks
- `Straight Ashi` often leads to straight ankle locks or outside heel exposure attempts

## Simple "How To Think About Leg Locks" Model

Most leg locks in BJJ fall into one of these categories:

- straight extension of the ankle
- rotation of the foot / ankle
- rotation through the heel into the knee
- straight extension of the knee
- compression of the calf / hamstring
- splitting pressure through the hips / groin

That is why many different leg locks feel related even when the names are different.

## Important Modeling Note

Leg locks should probably not be built as one giant flat list of:

- submissions
- entanglements
- finishing variations
- conceptual mechanics

Safer structure:

1. `Core submission` layer
2. `Submission family` layer
3. `Entanglement / control position` layer
4. `Variant / finish detail` layer
5. `Breaking mechanic / conceptual` layer

That way the Index can support:

- clean taxonomy
- better escape mapping
- better relationship mapping
- and future `Decision Tree` branching

without treating every variation like a brand-new submission.
